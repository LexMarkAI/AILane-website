Employment contracts for charities — what the law expects

This page assembles what the law expects a charity employment arrangement to address. It is groundwork for your solicitor — not contract wording to copy, and not legal advice.

What the law expects a charity employment arrangement to address. This page assembles requirements and considerations — groundwork for your solicitor, not contract wording to copy, and not legal advice.

Every paid employee
  • written statement of particulars from day one (Employment Rights Act 1996);
  • National Minimum Wage / National Living Wage (£12.71 for 21+ from April 2026);
  • working time and rest;
  • itemised pay;
  • pensions auto-enrolment where eligible;
  • right-to-work checks before start;
  • equality duties (Equality Act 2010);
  • disciplinary and grievance procedures;
  • for roles in regulated activity with children — enhanced DBS as a condition, safeguarding policy compliance as a contractual duty, safer-recruitment evidence on file, and training requirements with renewal cycles.
Workers (casual / event staff)
  • status follows reality, not the label — mutuality of obligation and control create rights;
  • NMW, holiday pay and working-time limits apply;
  • event-staff terms should address safeguarding expectations, supervision ratios where children attend, health and safety duties, and licensing-sensitive duties (alcohol service, door management).
Volunteers
  • no consideration beyond genuine expenses — payment, fixed "rewards" or obligations create worker status and back-pay risk;
  • use a volunteer agreement that states expectations without creating contractual obligation;
  • expenses documented receipts-only;
  • DBS where the role is regulated activity;
  • health and safety duties extend to volunteers (HSWA 1974 s.3).
Charity-specific overlays for any engagement
  • safeguarding clauses aligned to the charity's policy set;
  • duty-to-refer awareness (a criminal-offence-backed duty);
  • confidentiality boundaries that do not prevent statutory disclosures;
  • clarity that trustee duties are never delegated by contract;
  • insurance posture (employers' liability is compulsory where there are employees).
